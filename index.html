What Makes a Patio Door Truly Hurricane Rated?

A hurricane rated patio door is not a manufacturer's claim — it is a third-party test result with an approval number a building department can look up. The door has passed independent structural and impact tests, and the approval number is on record with the state or the insurer. If the number isn't there, the rating isn't real.

Design Pressure Rating — The Structural Wind Test

Design Pressure measures how much wind force a door's structure can resist, tested independently to a specific number. MP Doors' Impact Hinged Patio Door carries a DP50 rating — tested to +50/−50 pounds per square foot. This is not a manufacturer's estimate. It is a third-party test result that building inspectors and insurers use to verify the door meets local wind requirements.

Impact Glass Testing — ASTM E1886 and ASTM E1996

These two ASTM standards govern how impact glass is tested. ASTM E1886 covers the test method — large missile impact (a 9-pound 2×4 at 34 mph) followed by sustained cyclic wind pressure. ASTM E1996 sets the performance criteria. A door that passes both cannot be breached by windborne debris under those conditions. This is the same standard applied to hurricane shutters. MP Doors' impact glass is tested to both.

Florida Building Code Approval — The FL# Your Permit Needs

An FL# is a Florida Product Approval number assigned after independent testing, publicly searchable on floridabuilding.org. MP Doors' Impact Hinged Patio Door holds FBC# FL-29714. This is the approval number Florida building departments require when issuing windstorm-zone permits — including in the High Velocity Hurricane Zone. Sub-product 29714.2 carries explicit HVHZ approval covering Miami-Dade and Broward counties.

Texas Department of Insurance — The TDI Standard

Coastal Texas counties require separate windstorm documentation from the Texas Department of Insurance. TDI certification is what contractors need for windstorm-zone permit approval in TDI jurisdictions. The Impact Hinged Patio Door carries TDI certification alongside its Florida Building Code approval — both in a single composite door.

Composite vs. Vinyl — What Happens After the Permit Is Pulled

What Vinyl Does Over Time in a Coastal Climate

Vinyl frames expand and contract with heat cycling — a consistent process in Florida and Gulf Coast climates where temperatures swing significantly between seasons and between day and night. Sustained UV exposure causes surface chalking and increased brittleness over time. In salt-air environments, the repeated expansion and contraction cycling can compromise weatherstripping seals at the edges where the frame meets the sill and jamb. The structural rating is tested on day one; the material behaviour in year eight is a separate question.

What Composite Does Over Time in a Coastal Climate

Composite cannot absorb moisture — there is no mechanism for rot, delamination, or warping. The same HydroShield seal that exists on installation day exists in year fifteen, because composite does not cycle with moisture the way wood does or UV-degrade the way vinyl does. In coastal Florida and Texas Gulf Coast installations — sustained salt air, heat, and humidity — composite holds its geometry and its seal with no maintenance requirement tied to those conditions.

A hurricane rated door's certification is tested once, in a lab, on a specific day. The question for the coastal homeowner is what that door looks like in year ten, in the actual environment it was installed in. Composite answers that question differently than vinyl.

Common Questions About Hurricane Rated Patio Doors

Does MP Doors' patio door meet Florida building code? +
The MP Doors Impact Hinged Patio Door meets Florida Building Code requirements under approval number FBC# FL-29714. This is a Florida Product Approval number assigned after independent testing and verifiable directly on floridabuilding.org. Florida building departments require this number when issuing windstorm-zone permits — it is not a self-reported claim.
Is this door approved for the High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ)? +
The Impact Hinged Patio Door is approved for use in the High Velocity Hurricane Zone under FL29714-R15, sub-product 29714.2. HVHZ covers Miami-Dade and Broward counties — the most stringent hurricane zone in the Florida Building Code. The approval references TAS 201, TAS 202, and TAS 203, the Miami-Dade-specific HVHZ test protocols, in addition to ASTM E1886 and E1996. The approval is current as of November 2025 and publicly verifiable at floridabuilding.org.
Does the Impact Hinged door qualify for Texas windstorm permits? +
The Impact Hinged Patio Door is TDI certified by the Texas Department of Insurance for coastal Texas counties under TDI windstorm zone jurisdiction. The Impact Hinged holds both FBC and TDI certification — the documentation a coastal Texas permit requires — in a single composite door.
What is DP50 and why does it matter? +
Design Pressure 50 is an independently tested structural rating measuring how much wind force a door's frame and assembly can resist — +50/−50 pounds per square foot. It is not a manufacturer's estimate; it is a third-party test result. Building inspectors and insurers use this number to verify the door meets local wind requirements. The Impact Hinged Patio Door carries a DP50 rating.
What is ASTM E1886 and ASTM E1996? +
ASTM E1886 and ASTM E1996 are the two standards governing impact glass testing. E1886 covers the test method — large missile impact (a 9-pound 2×4 at 34 mph) followed by sustained cyclic wind pressure that simulates a hurricane. E1996 sets the performance criteria the product must meet. This is the same standard applied to hurricane shutters and storm-rated windows. MP Doors' impact glass is tested and approved to both standards.
What is the difference between DP50 and PG50? +
DP50 (Design Pressure 50) is a wind load structural rating used in Florida Building Code certification and impact door testing — it is the rating carried by the Impact Hinged Patio Door. PG50 (Performance Grade 50) is a rating from the AAMA/WDMA standards, more commonly used for windows and sliding doors — it is the rating carried by the Composite PG50 Sliding Patio Door for coastal applications. Both are independently tested ratings; they come from different standards frameworks.
Why is composite better than vinyl for a hurricane rated door? +
A composite door cannot absorb moisture — there is no mechanism for rot, warping, or delamination at the frame edges. In coastal environments, composite holds its geometry and seal integrity over time because the material does not cycle with moisture or UV-degrade the way vinyl does. A vinyl frame's structural rating is tested on day one; in year eight in a salt-air environment, the expansion and contraction cycling that vinyl undergoes can affect seal performance at the sill and jamb. The certification is the same; what differs is what each material does after it is installed.
How does the impact glass hold up during a hurricane? +
The Impact Hinged Patio Door uses 1-inch triple-layer LoE impact glass with a protective interlayer. In an impact event — windborne debris striking the glass — the interlayer bonds the glass so it does not shatter into shards. The glass may crack, but the opening stays closed. Wind and water do not enter through a compromised panel. This is what impact rated glass means: not shatter-proof, but breach-resistant. The glass is tested to ASTM E1886 and E1996, and to TAS 201, 202, and 203 for HVHZ applications.
What warranty covers the impact glass? +
The impact glass seal on the Impact Hinged Patio Door carries a 10-year prorated warranty. This is distinct from MP Doors' standard (non-impact) doors, which carry a 20-year prorated glass seal warranty — the difference is in the impact glass construction itself. Frame, panels, and astragals carry a Limited Lifetime Warranty for the original purchaser in an owner-occupied single-family home. The locking mechanism carries a 10-year warranty. Labor is not covered under any warranty term.
